Our Work

White papers and reports

  • Study of voting systems for Santa Clara, CA, an MGGG white paper on recommended remedies for a challenged at-large City Council voting scheme
  • Outlier analysis for Pennsylvania, expert report for LWV v. Commonwealth of Pennsylvania. (Typos corrected from filing.)

Tools and interactives

  • Gridlandia, an overview of the combinatorics of redistricting and the principles of Markov chain Monte Carlo, or MCMC.
  • GerryChain, our open-source python-based tool for MCMC on districting plans. A user guide is in development.
  • Ecological inference (EI) is the main statistical technique used to establish racially polarized voting (RPV).
    • Interactive introduction to EI
    • 2x2 tool, for two racial groups and two candidates;
    • RxC tool, for more groups and candidates.

Technical research

  • M.Duchin and B.Tenner, Discrete geometry for electoral geography, preprint. An overview of problems with "compactness" and a call for research on discrete scores.
  • D.DeFord, H.Lavenant, Z.Schutzman, and J.Solomon, Total variation isoperimetric profiles, preprint. An investigation of multi-scale compactness scores.
  • R.Barnes and J.Solomon, Gerrymandering and compactness: Implementation flexibility and abuse, preprint. Sensitivity analysis of compactness scores.
  • A.Bar-Natan, M.Duchin, and R.Kropholler, Medium-scale curvature for Cayley graphs, preprint. A pure math riff on an idea for gerrymandering: can we use Ricci curvature to detect natural clusters in the dual graphs of states and other jurisdictions?

Data and software repositories

  • Ohio precinct shapefile — we contacted all 88 counties in Ohio and collected information about the locations of voting precincts, then digitized them and produced a publicly available shapefile, the first for that state.
  • Discrete compactness hosts a collection of data and code to study the relationships between different ideas for graph-based compactness scores
  • BVAP explorer, a jupyter notebook for studying the relationship between Black voting age percentage (BVAP) and patterns of current Black congressional representation.

Related work by friends of MGGG

  • All About Redistricting, the excellent website of redistricting law scholar Justin Levitt. The best place to read about what rules are in place in what places around the country. (In the process of a reboot.)
  • MGGG consulted on Eric Lander’s amicus brief for Gill v Whitford
  • The CMU/Pitt gerrymandering team has an article providing methods for drawing rigorous statistical conclusions from random walks (published in PNAS). They provide code in a public repository.
  • The Duke gerrymandering team led by Jonathan Mattingly has several resources available that highlight their MCMC approach.
    • North Carolina analysis;
    • Wisconsin analysis.
  • Can Math Help the Supreme Court Free Congress from Gerrymanders?, Karen Saxe, Notices of the American Mathematical Society, Jan 2018
  • The ACLU’s pamphlet Everything You Always Wanted to Know about Redistricting (But Were Afraid to Ask!), updated 2010— this is a wonderful place to learn about some of the basic terms and notions, but is now a little out of date (considering Shelby v Holder).
  • The geospatial application company Azavea has published a white paper on redistricting. (A good general introduction to different compactness measures but slightly mathematically buggy.)
